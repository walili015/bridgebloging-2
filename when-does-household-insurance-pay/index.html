When does household insurance pay?

A break-in in your own apartment – a horrific idea. You come home from work and the door is open. Inside there is a lot of chaos and you quickly realize: I was broken into! The first question that comes up often afterwards: Does my household insurance pay? LAMIE has summarized the most important requirements for you.

The danger of falling victim to a burglary is omnipresent. According to the Federal Criminal Police Office, around 25 break-ins occur every day in Austria. In these cases, those affected naturally hope for help from household insurance. In order for this to compensate for the damage, however, the following requirements must be met:

Condition 1: There must be burglary or robbery

If your belongings are stolen from your own four walls, the HOW is the most important thing when it comes to household insurance. Usually only burglary and robbery are insured.

Burglary means that the thief breaks into locked or locked rooms. As follows:

  • by breaking open doors, windows or other parts of the building
  • by entering through openings that are not intended for entry (by overcoming significant obstacles)
  • by breaking locks with tools or the wrong key
  • with correct keys that were obtained through burglary in other than the insured premises or through the use or threat of violence

 Do not keep apartment keys in the basement or other insurance space outside the apartment. If the burglar breaks in to get the key, the items stolen from the apartment are not insured.

Damage caused by vandalism after a break-in is usually not covered, but can be included as an additional module.

On the other hand, robbery occurs when you are forced to pull out your belongings at home by using or threatening to use force – and without a break-in. This is the case, for example, if you are forced to open the entrance door.

Requirement 2: There must be no simple theft

The most common exclusion from insurance is simple theft . This is the case if the thief had unhindered access to your personal belongings. So there is usually no cover if you leave your apartment unattended – even if only for a short time – with the windows, balcony or doors open. The so-called creeping theft is also usually not insured. A sneak thief is someone who sneaks into the apartment unnoticed and sneaks out or breaks out again unnoticed.

Don’t forget: If there is no insurance cover for simple theft at home, then there is none outside your own four walls. (e.g. if your bag is simply torn away in the supermarket or in the café)

 In some cases there are insurance companies that also insure simple or creeping theft. However, you shouldn’t be surprised if the insurer refuses cover on the grounds of gross negligence. Insurance against simple theft – sounds good, but usually doesn’t help!

Requirement 3: It is not allowed to act “grossly negligent”

One of the most popular reasons for rejection from insurance companies is the subject of “gross negligence”. According to the general definition, someone acts with gross negligence if he neglects the required degree of care! This statement alone is likely to provide material for hours of discussion. According to our experts, however, the following are particularly critical when it comes to burglary:

  • The door only snaps into place when leaving the apartment (if all residents leave the apartment, the apartment door must be locked with the key in any case. It is not enough to just close a door with a knob on the outside).
  • Tilted windows on the first floor
  • Outdoor climbing aids
  • Hiding keys outside the building (e.g. under the doormat)

 Tip: Pay attention to the inclusion of gross negligence in your insurance cover.

Requirement 4: Valuables must be kept in the safe

Household insurance usually covers everyday objects. Valuables such as cash, savings books, jewelry, etc. are also insured in the event of a break-in, but the benefits are often linked to certain conditions. For example, some insurance companies only replace valuables if they have been kept in a safe. In addition, the maximum values ​​that can be kept in a safe depend on certain security classes. Wall safes must also be walled in according to regulations.

Warning: do not use a key safe! Some insurance companies have special regulations on how and where the key must be kept. In the case of safekeeping within the insurance premises, it is prescribed, for example, that the key must be stored in an equally secure container as the safe itself. Translated, this would mean that you need your own safe just for the safe key. A safe with mechanical or electronic locking saves you from worrying.

In addition, there are usually certain maximum compensation limits (sublimits) for exposed and locked valuables. At best, you can increase this by adding a premium.

Tip: Document your valuables and belongings well and keep the bills. Also save an electronic copy of it externally (in case your computer is stolen). This facilitates the processing in the event of damage immensely.

Prerequisite 5: Police report and forensic evidence are a MUST

If you become the victim of a break-in or robbery, report it to the police immediately (but no longer than 48 hours). This then carries out forensic evidence on site. The insurer can then use the police report to understand what happened.

If the report is filed late, it could be that traces are obliterated or destroyed and the police can no longer determine the cause and the cause of the damage. In such a case, the insurance may refuse to pay.

Do not forget:

  • Locks can only be exchanged, windows repaired and chairs set up again after they have been reported. However, this no-trace-blurring rule does not apply if damage caused by the burglar can still be reduced. So if you have a fire or the bathroom has been flooded, you are subject to the so-called damage mitigation obligation.
  • Notify your insurer too! This also wants to be informed immediately and comprehensively about the claim. Late reporting of a claim can also lead to the insurer being exempt from providing benefits.

Requirement 6: Reported safety devices such as alarm systems must be activated

Insurance companies usually grant low premium discounts for reported alarm systems or security doors. All well and good, but can be expensive in the event of damage. If, for example, the alarm system is deactivated during a break-in, insurance cover is no longer applicable. So make sure that all additionally agreed security measures are observed.

 In case of doubt, don’t tick the box for an alarm system, so you are always protected.
